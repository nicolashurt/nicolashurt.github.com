3-D Cinema: Triumph of the Image

3-D cinema arose as the product of a midlife crisis in the 1950's, and it was quickly married to the exploitation genre. Here I explore its relation to the earlier "cinema of attractions" and contemporary Hollywood cinema through the manners in which the image either compliments or competes with story.

Psychoanalytic Theory and A Clockwork Orange

Stanley Kubrick's A Clockwork Orange (1971) explores the debate of free will's role in morality. Alex's transformation throughout the film can best be understood in the context of psychoanalytic theory.

Citizen Kane and the Active Spectator

Orson Welles' Citizen Kane (1941) explores cinema's potential to communicate visual meaning through a dynamic spectator-screen relationship. The film's sophisticated use of deep focus presents multiple, interacting planes of interest within a shot, demanding a more active spectatorship.

The Gangster Film: A Welcomed Tragedy

Epitomized by Howard Hawks' Scarface (1932), the gangster film genre provided spectators with cathartic relief from the social tensions brought about by the Great Depression, and it presented a critical discourse on American mass culture.

Batman: Critical Reception of the Hollywood Blockbuster

Tim Burton's Batman (1989) employs an awe-inspiring visual style to attract its audience, ultimately substituting spectacle for narrative consistency, a concession that is reflected by its disparaging critical reception.
